# Defunct Crypto Cards & Banks Research

Last Updated: 2025-02-07

## Overview
This document compiles research on defunct, shuttered, and failed crypto card and banking projects. These serve as cautionary tales for users evaluating current crypto card offerings.

---

## 1. BlockFi Rewards Credit Card

**What it was:** 
- Visa Signature Credit Card offering 1.5% cashback in Bitcoin on all purchases
- Up to 3.5% back on first $100 spent each month
- 2% on stablecoin purchases, 10% on interest payments
- No annual fee

**When it closed:** November 2022

**Why:**
- FTX collapse triggered BlockFi's bankruptcy (FTX had extended $400M credit line)
- Filed Chapter 11 bankruptcy on November 28, 2022
- Card purchases suspended on November 11, 2022

**What happened to users:**
- Card holders couldn't make purchases immediately
- Existing balances still owed to issuing bank (Deserve)
- Crypto rewards stuck in BlockFi accounts
- Users received partial recovery through bankruptcy proceedings

**Lessons:**
- Even credit cards (not prepaid) can be affected by issuer bankruptcy
- Rewards held by a third party are at risk
- Watch for signs of financial distress in partners

## 2. Voyager Digital

**What it was:**
- Cryptocurrency brokerage and exchange
- Offered a debit card program
- FDIC-insured claims (later found to be misleading)
- Competitive crypto trading with VGX token rewards

**When it closed:** July 2022

**Why:**
- Three Arrows Capital (3AC) exposure
- Crypto market crash
- Filed Chapter 11 on July 6, 2022
- FTC charged them for falsely claiming FDIC insurance

**What happened to users:**
- Trading, deposits, withdrawals, and loyalty awards suspended
- Users blocked from accessing funds
- Customers received ~35-73% of their crypto back through bankruptcy
- Some users lost life savings

**Lessons:**
- "FDIC insured" claims need scrutiny — crypto deposits aren't FDIC covered
- Counterparty risk is real
- Don't keep life savings in one platform

## 3. Celsius Network

**What it was:**
- Crypto lending platform with planned credit card
- Up to 17% APY on crypto deposits
- CEL token for boosted rewards
- "Unbank yourself" marketing

**When it closed:** July 2022

**Why:**
- Liquidity crisis from crypto market crash
- Risky lending practices exposed
- Filed Chapter 11 on July 13, 2022
- CEO Alex Mashinsky arrested for fraud

**What happened to users:**
- Withdrawals frozen on June 12, 2022
- Users couldn't access their crypto
- Bankruptcy proceedings returned ~60% to creditors
- Credit card never launched

**Lessons:**
- High yields often mean high risk
- "Too good to be true" rates are a red flag
- Centralized lenders carry counterparty risk

## 4. FTX Exchange

**What it was:**
- Third-largest crypto exchange globally
- Offered FTX Card (Visa debit card)
- Zero fees on purchases
- Integrated with FTX US accounts

**When it closed:** November 2022

**Why:**
- $8 billion hole in accounts
- Customer funds misused
- Sam Bankman-Fried fraud
- Filed bankruptcy November 11, 2022

**What happened to users:**
- Immediate freeze on withdrawals
- Card stopped working
- Users are receiving 100%+ recovery in bankruptcy (due to crypto price increases since 2022)
- Criminal prosecution of executives

**Lessons:**
- Even "reputable" exchanges can collapse
- Not your keys, not your crypto
- Avoid keeping large balances on exchanges

## 5. Genesis Global

**What it was:**
- Major crypto lender
- Institutional-grade services
- Part of Digital Currency Group (DCG)
- Offered high-yield products

**When it closed:** January 2023

**Why:**
- Fallout from FTX collapse
- Exposure to 3AC and Alameda
- Filed Chapter 11 on January 19, 2023

**What happened to users:**
- Withdrawals halted November 2022
- Returned ~$3 billion to customers
- Parent company DCG received no recovery

**Lessons:**
- Interconnected crypto firms create systemic risk
- Even "institutional grade" can fail
- Yield products carry hidden risks

## 6. Shift Card

**What it was:**
- First Coinbase-connected Visa debit card
- Spend BTC directly from Coinbase account
- Available in US
- Pioneered crypto spending cards

**When it closed:** April 11, 2019

**Why:**
- Business decision, not bankruptcy
- Regulatory challenges
- Market conditions
- Company pivoted focus

**What happened to users:**
- Cards deactivated on deadline
- Funds remained safe in Coinbase accounts
- Users had to find alternative cards

**Lessons:**
- Even solvent companies can discontinue products
- Card programs can end without warning
- Keep alternatives available

## 7. WaveCrest / EU Crypto Card Apocalypse

**What it was:**
- WaveCrest Holdings was the primary Visa card issuer for most crypto cards in EU
- Powered: TenX, Wirex, Bitwala, and dozens of others

**When it closed:** January 2018

**Why:**
- Visa revoked WaveCrest's license
- Compliance issues with card issuance rules
- Affected entire EU crypto card market overnight

**What happened to users:**
- All WaveCrest-issued cards immediately cancelled
- Users had to transfer funds back
- Many crypto card startups scrambled to find new issuers
- Wirex and others eventually recovered with new partners

**Lessons:**
- Single point of failure in card issuance is dangerous
- Know who issues your card
- Regulatory risk can strike suddenly

## 8. Nuri (formerly Bitwala)

**What it was:**
- German crypto bank
- Full IBAN bank accounts with crypto trading
- Visa debit card
- Bitcoin and Ethereum integration

**When it closed:** October 2022

**Why:**
- Market downturn affected business
- Failed to raise funds or find acquirer
- Filed insolvency August 9, 2022
- "Tough economical & political environment"

**What happened to users:**
- Fiat funds protected (German banking partner)
- Crypto needed to be transferred out
- Company shut down entirely in October 2022

**Lessons:**
- European banking regulations provided some protection
- Crypto-bank hybrids face unique challenges
- Market downturns kill startups without runway

## 9. MCO Token (Crypto.com)

**What it was:**
- Original token powering Crypto.com cards
- Required staking for card tiers
- Separate from CRO token

**When it closed:** November 2, 2020

**Why:**
- Crypto.com consolidated to single token (CRO)
- Swap program offered (1 MCO = 27.64 CRO)
- Business restructuring

**What happened to users:**
- Mandatory swap to CRO
- Some users felt exchange rate was unfair
- Auto-unstaking after deadline
- Card benefits continued under CRO

**Lessons:**
- Token economics can change overnight
- Platform tokens carry platform risk
- Read tokenomics carefully

## 10. TenX (PAY Token)

**What it was:**
- Singapore-based crypto card
- PAY token with promised rewards
- Raised $80M in 2017 ICO
- Celebrity endorsements (Julian Hosp)

**When it closed:** ~2020-2021

**Why:**
- WaveCrest shutdown in 2018 hurt operations
- Failed to deliver on promises
- PAY token crashed
- Founder controversies

**What happened to users:**
- Card program failed to relaunch properly
- PAY token became nearly worthless
- ICO investors lost most of their investment
- Singapore operations wound down

**Lessons:**
- ICO promises often go unfulfilled
- Celebrity/influencer backing means nothing
- Cards built on third-party issuers are fragile

## 11. Swipe (SXP)

**What it was:**
- Crypto Visa card with SXP token staking
- Up to 4% cashback
- Acquired by Binance in 2020

**Status:** Largely defunct/absorbed

**What happened:**
- Binance acquired Swipe
- Card program merged into Binance Card
- SXP token continues but card is discontinued
- Original Swipe card holders migrated

**Lessons:**
- Acquisitions can kill products
- Token can survive even if product doesn't
- Corporate changes affect users

## Summary: Common Failure Patterns

### 1. Counterparty Risk
BlockFi, Voyager, Celsius, FTX — all failed due to bad loans, fraud, or exposure to failed entities.

### 2. Regulatory Risk
WaveCrest, Shift — card programs shut down due to regulatory/licensing issues.

### 3. Market Conditions
Nuri, TenX — couldn't survive crypto winter without adequate funding.

### 4. Token Economics
MCO, SXP — tokens changed or deprecated, affecting card programs.

### 5. Single Points of Failure
WaveCrest affecting dozens of cards shows systemic risk.

---

## Red Flags to Watch For

1. **Unsustainably high yields** — If it seems too good to be true, it is
2. **Opaque financials** — Can't verify how they make money
3. **Single issuer dependency** — Ask who issues the card
4. **Token-gated features** — Your access depends on token value
5. **Aggressive marketing** — Celebrities, influencers, sponsorships
6. **Custody concerns** — Who actually holds your funds?
7. **Regulatory uncertainty** — Operating in gray areas
8. **Concentrated counterparty exposure** — Who are they lending to?
9. **Founder red flags** — Legal issues, past failures
10. **Rapid growth without infrastructure** — Scaling too fast
